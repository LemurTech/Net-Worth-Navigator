---
title: Understanding Monte Carlo Analysis
description: How to interpret probability bands, success rates, and failure metrics
---

Monte Carlo simulation runs your scenario thousands of times with randomized annual returns. The result isn't a single prediction — it's a range of possible outcomes. This page explains how to read and use that information.

## What Monte Carlo Tells You

A deterministic projection says: *"If returns are exactly X% every year, here's your net worth."*

A Monte Carlo projection says: *"Given your assumptions about volatility, here's the range of outcomes you might see, and how likely each one is."*

The key outputs are:

- **Probability of Success** — what percentage of simulations never ran out of money
- **The shaded band chart** — showing the range from pessimistic (10th percentile) to optimistic (90th percentile)
- **The median line** — the middle-of-the-road outcome (50th percentile)

## Reading the Band Chart

The main Monte Carlo chart shows your net worth trajectory as shaded regions:

- **Darkest band** (center) — the 25th to 75th percentile range. Half of all simulated outcomes fell within this zone.
- **Lighter bands** — extend to the 10th and 90th percentiles. 80% of outcomes fall within the full shaded area.
- **Solid line** — the median (50th percentile) outcome.

A wide band means high uncertainty — small changes in returns produce very different outcomes. A narrow band means the outcome is relatively predictable.

## Understanding Success and Failure

Success is defined by the `failure_mode` in your scenario's configuration:

| Failure Mode | What Counts as Failure |
|---|---|
| `net_worth_below_zero` | Total net worth drops below zero |
| `liquid_depletion` | Available cash and investments are exhausted |
| `spending_shortfall` | Can't meet the spending target |
| `preserve_home_equity` | Must sell the home to cover spending |

A 90% probability of success means that in 9 out of 10 simulations, the plan never triggered the failure condition. This does **not** mean there's a 10% chance of going broke — it means that under 10% of the random return sequences tested, the plan didn't hold up.

## Key Metrics in the Simulation Tab

| Metric | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Median Terminal NW** | The middle outcome — half did better, half did worse |
| **Worst-Decile Terminal NW** | The 10th percentile outcome. If you're planning conservatively, plan for this. |
| **First Failure Year** | The earliest year where any simulation failed. Tells you your risk window. |
| **Peak Failure Rate** | The highest percentage of simulations failing in a single year. A spike here identifies a vulnerable period. |

## Tips for Using Monte Carlo

- **Start with deterministic** — get your assumptions right in deterministic mode first
- **Increase `num_runs`** for smoother bands — the default is 1000, but 5000+ gives cleaner percentiles
- **Check the failure timing** — if failures cluster in a specific year (e.g., right after retirement), that's your risk period
- **Use `failure_grace_period_months`** — a 6-month grace period prevents temporary dips from counting as failures
- **Compare scenarios in deterministic mode** — Monte Carlo adds stochastic noise that can obscure scenario differences

## When Not to Use Monte Carlo

- **Initial scenario design** — use deterministic, it's simpler and clearer
- **Comparing two scenarios** — use deterministic; stochastic noise can make small differences invisible
- **Presenting to a partner** — deterministic is easier to explain
